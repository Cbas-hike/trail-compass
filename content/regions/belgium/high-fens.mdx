The High Fens (Hautes Fagnes) is Europe's most extensive highland bog—a 4,000+ hectare plateau at 600+ meters elevation with an almost Scandinavian character despite being in Western Europe. This is a genuinely rare ecosystem. Wooden boardwalk systems protect the fragile sphagnum moss while allowing visitor access. The landscape is utterly unique: open, windswept, with rare botanical species and migratory bird populations.

Signal de Botrange (694 meters) is Belgium's highest point—though unlike mountain peaks elsewhere, there's no dramatic vista, just open bog stretching to the horizon. On clear days, visibility extends into Germany and the Netherlands. The modest elevation creates dramatic weather patterns—mist can roll in within minutes, or sudden winds can gust unexpectedly strong. The plateau's exposure means full weather immersion rather than sheltered forest hiking.

The bog ecology is remarkable. Sphagnum moss species cover the ground, creating the underlying peat structure—a carbon sink that stores millennia of plant material. Carnivorous sundew plants trap insects in sticky red leaves. Rare orchids appear seasonally. The bird population is exceptional—migratory species use the plateau as staging ground, year-round residents have adapted to extreme conditions. Early morning hikes offer excellent birdwatching opportunities.

The wooden boardwalk network is Europe's most extensive bog walkway system. Originally built to protect the ecosystem from human impact, the boardwalks also protect hikers' feet from the perpetually waterlogged terrain. They're narrow (1 meter wide) and occasionally slick in rain, adding a subtle adventure element.

Lac de Robertville is an artificial reservoir that adds water features to the plateau's landscape. The lake offers a sheltered location for rest and lunch. Fagne de la Polleur boardwalk section is particularly rewarding botanically.

The region has an almost Scandinavian character—peat bogs, wind exposure, open moorland, weather drama, and botanical uniqueness more reminiscent of Iceland or northern Scotland than Belgium. This character makes it unique in Western Europe. The visitor center (Signal de Botrange) offers museum exhibits explaining the bog ecology and natural history, providing context that enhances appreciation of the landscape.

Weather predictability is low—expect rapid changes. Spring (April-May) can still snow. Summer is the most accessible season. Autumn offers beautiful light and moderate weather. Winter is challenging for most hikers. Regardless of season, waterproof gear and wind protection are essential, not optional.
