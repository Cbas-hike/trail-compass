Zuid-Limburg is the Netherlands' most dramatic landscape—an entirely different character from the flat agricultural lands that define the rest of the country. Real elevation gain (up to 520m total), narrow river valleys carved through limestone bedrock, medieval castle ruins perched impossibly on cliff tops, and rolling wine country create a landscape more reminiscent of the Ardennes or Alsace than the Low Countries.

Drielandenpunt (321 meters) is the Netherlands' highest point, and marks where three nations converge—you can literally stand in the Netherlands, Belgium, and Germany simultaneously. The monument is modest, but the vista across three-country borderland from this vantage point is extraordinary. The descent from Drielandenpunt into the Geuldal is the region's most dramatic hiking section.

The Geuldal (Geul Valley) is a limestone gorge carved by the Geul river, creating a landscape of towering limestone cliffs, dense beech forests, and a narrow river corridor. The medieval town of Valkenburg clings to a limestone promontory, with castle ruins visible for kilometers around. In autumn, the beech forests turn golden and russet, creating unforgettable scenery. Spring brings wildflowers—cowslips, primrose, and wild garlic carpet the valley floor.

Sint-Pietersberg is a limestone plateau dotted with caves and marl quarries. The plateau itself offers excellent hiking with views across the farmland below. The caves are historically significant—some dating to Roman times, others expanded during medieval periods.

The rolling upland farmland is covered with vineyards (yes, Dutch wine production centers here), fruit orchards (cherries, plums, apples), and tobacco fields creating a cultural landscape unchanged for centuries. Small villages retain medieval architecture and narrow streets. Wine estates and cideries offer tastings and overnight accommodations.

This region attracts more serious hikers than the Veluwe, with technical terrain, sustained elevation changes, and challenging ascents. The Geuldal routes rival Alpine hiking despite modest elevations. Autumn is peak season—golden foliage, comfortable temperatures, and manageable crowds. Spring is equally beautiful but potentially muddy. Excellent train and bus connectivity links all major hiking towns to Maastricht, Liège, and beyond. Multiple multi-day hiking routes (MAW network) connect this region to Belgium and Germany.
